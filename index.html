About the project

Detecting socioeconomic gradients and ethnic differences in severe outcomes after respiratory viral infections in England.

Aims

  • Quantify associations between socioeconomic status (SES), ethnicity, and severe disease (hospitalisation or death) for COVID‑19, influenza, and RSV.
  • Investigate differences in age at severe event by SES and ethnicity.
  • Test for interactions between SES and ethnicity and explore potential clustering of infections across diseases.

Why it matters

Those with greater social disadvantage often bear a greater burden of disease. Understanding gradients and intersections can guide vaccine programmes and services to protect the most vulnerable — with benefits for equity, productivity, and healthcare capacity.

Data & setting

  • OpenSAFELY secure analytics platform, accessing NHS EHR for ~58 million people in England.
  • Exposures: SES (IMD quintiles, subject to refinement) and ethnicity (higher‑level categories, guided by stakeholders).
  • Outcome: Severe disease — hospitalisation or death attributed to infection.

Methods

  • Variable scoping and literature review.
  • Directed acyclic graph (DAG) to inform adjustment sets.
  • Descriptive profiles across SES and ethnic groups.
  • Regression: linear/logistic models; SES tested as categorical and continuous; SES×ethnicity interaction.
  • Time‑to‑event: Cox regression (with PH diagnostics) and Kaplan–Meier.
  • Missing data: pattern assessment; complete‑case analysis where appropriate.

Limitations & challenges

  • Under‑coverage of people experiencing homelessness or undocumented migration in EHR.
  • Potential bias in infection incidence due to variable testing rates.
  • Limitations of clinical coding for conditions such as long COVID.

Impact & involvement

Short‑term: sharper vaccine targeting and risk mitigation. Long‑term: evidence to investigate specific drivers behind SES and ethnicity effects. Stakeholder input for meaningful categories and transparency via public‑facing feedback routes.
